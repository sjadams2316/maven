## 1. What is IRMAA?

**Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount (IRMAA)** is a surcharge added to Medicare Part B and Part D premiums for higher-income beneficiaries. It was established by the Medicare Modernization Act of 2003 and began affecting Part B premiums in 2007 and Part D premiums in 2011.

### Key Points:
- **NOT a tax** ‚Äî it's an additional premium amount
- Affects approximately **8% of Medicare beneficiaries**
- Applies to **both** Medicare Part B and Part D
- Paid **monthly** (deducted from Social Security or paid directly)
- Determined by **Social Security Administration (SSA)** based on IRS data
- Uses a **two-year lookback** to prior tax returns

### The Surprise Factor:
High earners often don't anticipate IRMAA because:
1. They weren't paying attention to Medicare costs pre-65
2. The two-year lookback means income from years ago affects current premiums
3. One-time income events (Roth conversions, stock sales) can push them into higher brackets
4. The "cliff" effect ‚Äî going $1 over a threshold costs the full surcharge for the bracket

## 3. The Two-Year Lookback Period

### How It Works

IRMAA for any given year is based on your **Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI)** from **two years prior**:

| Medicare Year | Tax Year Used |
|---|---|
| 2025 | 2023 tax return |
| 2026 | 2024 tax return |
| 2027 | 2025 tax return |

### Why Two Years?
- SSA needs finalized IRS data
- Tax returns aren't filed until April (or later with extensions)
- IRS data exchange with SSA takes time

### Implications for Planning
- **Retirement timing matters**: Income in your last working year affects Medicare premiums two years later
- **Roth conversions**: Large conversions in years 63-65 impact premiums at 65-67
- **Stock sales/bonuses**: One-time income spikes have two-year delayed impact
- **Planning window**: You need to look ahead 2+ years when making income decisions

## 4. MAGI Calculation for IRMAA

### IRMAA MAGI = AGI + Tax-Exempt Interest

The MAGI used for IRMAA is simpler than other MAGI calculations:

**MAGI = Adjusted Gross Income (Line 11 of Form 1040) + Tax-Exempt Interest (Line 2a of Form 1040)**

### What's INCLUDED in IRMAA MAGI:

| Income Type | Included? |
|---|---|
| Wages and salaries | ‚úÖ Yes |
| Self-employment income | ‚úÖ Yes |
| Taxable IRA/401(k) distributions | ‚úÖ Yes |
| Pension income | ‚úÖ Yes |
| Capital gains (including LTCG) | ‚úÖ Yes |
| Taxable Social Security benefits | ‚úÖ Yes |
| Rental income | ‚úÖ Yes |
| Business income (Schedule C, K-1) | ‚úÖ Yes |
| **Tax-exempt municipal bond interest** | ‚úÖ Yes (this surprises people!) |
| Dividends | ‚úÖ Yes |
| Interest income | ‚úÖ Yes |

### What's NOT Included:

| Income Type | Included? |
|---|---|
| Roth IRA distributions (qualified) | ‚ùå No |
| Return of basis from non-qualified accounts | ‚ùå No |
| Loans (HELOC, 401k loans, etc.) | ‚ùå No |

| HSA distributions for qualified expenses | ‚ùå No |

### Key Insight:
**Roth distributions don't count toward IRMAA MAGI** ‚Äî this is why Roth conversions before Medicare are so valuable, despite the tension (see Section 7).

## 5. Life-Changing Events That Allow Appeals

If you experience certain life-changing events, you can request that SSA use more recent income data instead of the two-year lookback. This is done via **Form SSA-44**.

### Qualifying Life-Changing Events:

1. **Marriage**
2. **Divorce or annulment**
3. **Death of a spouse**
4. **Work stoppage** (retirement, layoff, reduced hours)
5. **Work reduction** (significant decrease in income)
6. **Loss of income-producing property** (disaster, foreclosure, etc.)
7. **Loss of pension income** (pension termination)
8. **Employer settlement payment** (severance due to employer's closure/bankruptcy)

### Appeal Process:

1. **Complete Form SSA-44** (Medicare Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount ‚Äì Life-Changing Event)
2. Provide documentation proving the life-changing event
3. Provide estimate of current/future income
4. Submit to local Social Security office

### Documentation Required:
- Death certificate (spouse's death)
- Signed statement from employer (work stoppage)
- Letter from pension administrator (pension loss)
- Court documentation (divorce)
- Marriage certificate (marriage)

### What Does NOT Qualify:
- üö´ Large one-time capital gain or stock sale
- üö´ Roth conversion (even if it pushed you over)
- üö´ Selling a business
- üö´ "I didn't know about IRMAA"

### Timing:
- Appeals can be filed **any time** during the year
- Approved appeals apply to the **current calendar year**
- May need to re-file annually if circumstances continue

## 6. Strategies to Stay Below Thresholds

### The "Cliff" Problem

IRMAA brackets are **hard cliffs**, not gradual increases:
- Going $1 over the $212,000 threshold (MFJ) costs ~$2,105/year extra
- The marginal "tax rate" on that $1 is effectively **infinite**

### Strategy 1: Income Timing/Bunching

**Bunch deductions in alternating years:**
- Take larger charitable deductions in one year to offset income
- Use Donor Advised Fund for "lumpy" charitable giving
- Time itemized vs. standard deduction strategically

### Strategy 2: Tax-Loss Harvesting Near Thresholds

**Realize losses strategically:**
- Offset gains that would push you over IRMAA thresholds
- More valuable than just reducing taxable income

### Strategy 3: Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs)

After age 70¬Ω:
- Donate directly from IRA to charity (up to $105,000 in 2025)
- QCD satisfies RMD but **does NOT appear in AGI**
- Perfect for those on the IRMAA edge

### Strategy 4: Roth Conversions BEFORE Age 63

**Front-load Roth conversions:**
- Convert heavily in years that won't affect Medicare
- Years 55-62 are the "conversion window" for many retirees
- Balance against other tax considerations

### Strategy 5: Asset Location for Income Control

**Place income-producing assets strategically:**
- High-yield bonds ‚Üí Traditional IRA (distributions are controllable)
- Growth stocks ‚Üí Taxable (control when gains realized)
- Municipal bonds ‚Üí Reconsider if near IRMAA threshold (they count!)

### Strategy 6: Shift Income to Non-IRMAA Sources

**Draw from Roth accounts** when near thresholds:
- Roth IRA distributions don't count toward MAGI
- Mix traditional and Roth withdrawals to manage MAGI

### Strategy 7: Consider Medicare Part B Deferral

If still working at 65 with employer coverage:
- Can delay Part B enrollment
- May avoid IRMAA during high-income years
- Must enroll within 8 months of losing employer coverage

### Strategy 8: Business Owner Strategies

**For small business owners:**
- Adjust timing of K-1 income if possible
- Consider S-corp salary vs distribution mix
- Time business sales carefully

## 7. Roth Conversions and IRMAA Planning Tension

### The Paradox

Roth conversions are excellent for tax diversification, but they:
1. **Increase current-year MAGI** ‚Üí potentially trigger IRMAA
2. Have a **two-year delayed impact** on Medicare premiums
3. Create tension between "convert now" and "avoid IRMAA"

### The Math

Example: $100,000 Roth conversion for someone at the threshold

**Costs:**
- Federal tax: $24,000 (24% bracket)
- State tax: ~$5,000 (varies)
- IRMAA for 2 years later: ~$2,105-$5,287 (depends on bracket jumped)
- **Total cost: ~$31,000-$34,000**

**Benefits:**
- Future tax-free growth
- No RMDs on Roth
- Tax-free income in retirement
- Estate planning benefits

### Optimal Strategy: The "Conversion Corridor"

**Ages 55-62**: Aggressive Roth conversions (no Medicare impact)
**Ages 63-64**: Moderate conversions (affects first Medicare years)
**Ages 65+**: Conservative conversions up to IRMAA thresholds

### When IRMAA Cost is Worth It

**Accept IRMAA when:**
- You expect to be in higher tax brackets later
- You have significant pre-tax assets ($1M+)
- You're in the 12%-22% bracket now (low conversion cost)
- You have decades of tax-free growth ahead
- Estate planning is a priority

**Avoid IRMAA when:**
- You're only slightly over the threshold
- The conversion would jump multiple IRMAA tiers
- You have limited time horizon
- You expect to be in lower brackets later

### The Numbers Game

**IRMAA Break-Even Analysis:**

If a $50,000 conversion triggers Tier 2 IRMAA (~$2,105/year for couple):
- One-time IRMAA cost: $2,105
- Required tax savings to break even: Need $2,105 / 0.12 = ~$17,500 less income taxed at 12% higher rate
- Time horizon matters: 20 years of tax-free growth often beats the one-time IRMAA hit
